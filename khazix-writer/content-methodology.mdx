## Topic Selection Model

### The Intersection Model

A good topic lives at the intersection of three things:

<CardGroup cols={3}>
  <Card title="Your Expertise" icon="brain">
    Pure expertise without reader appeal is too niche. You need the domain knowledge to provide real depth.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Reader Interest" icon="users">
    Pure reader interest without expertise produces shallow content. The overlap with what readers actually want is what makes it land.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Current Timing" icon="clock">
    Pure trend-chasing without expertise or genuine interest is a flash in the pan. Timing amplifies — it doesn't replace — substance.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

All three together find the most precise ignition point.

### HKR Quality Check

Every topic must pass the HKR test before writing begins.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="H — Happy / Interesting（有趣）">
    Is it interesting enough? Does it have suspense or unexpectedness? Would the title and opening make someone curious enough to click?

    A topic that scores H is one where readers think: "I have to know how this turns out." Topics with inherent drama, absurdity, or "you won't believe this" energy score here.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="K — Knowledge / Informative（有信息量）">
    Is there real information density? Will readers feel like they learned something by the end?

    Topics that score K provide a genuine insight, new data, an unexpected connection between things, or a practical skill. The reader's understanding of the world is different after reading.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="R — Resonance / Emotional（能共鸣）">
    Does it hit an emotion? Will readers feel "yes, exactly, that's what I think too"?

    Topics that score R touch something the reader has felt but hasn't articulated. It might be anxiety, excitement, nostalgia, the frustration of a shared experience, or the relief of being understood.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

<Note>
  S-tier topics score all three. Passing topics need at least two. A topic that scores only one should be reconsidered. A topic that scores zero should be abandoned.
</Note>

### Role Substitution Method for Topic Evaluation

When assessing whether a topic is worth writing, mentally audition it through three personas:

<Steps>

### "A Very Busy Ordinary User"
> Can this help me save time or solve a specific real problem?

If the answer is no, the topic may be too niche or too abstract to be practically valuable.

### "A Friend Who Loves to Play"
> Is this interesting enough? Will I get a "holy shit, you can do that?!" reaction?

If the answer is no, the topic may be too dry or too expected to be fun.

### "An Anxious Learner"
> Will I learn something new? Will this give me a new angle or new understanding?

If the answer is no, the topic may lack depth or bring nothing new to what already exists.

</Steps>

An S-tier topic satisfies at least two of the three personas, ideally all three.

## Content Rhythm

### Rhythm as a Push System

Rhythm is not a writing style choice — it is a structural mechanism that keeps readers moving forward without stopping.

<Tip>
  The benchmark creators for rhythm reference: 影视飓风, 何同学, 小狮日记, 毕导, 小Lin, 九边.
</Tip>

Good rhythm functions like a wave pattern:

```
Main line →  slight drift (example / case / breathing room)
         →  anchor sentence pulls back to main line
         →  advance forward
         →  slight drift again
         →  anchor sentence
         →  advance
```

The drift gives readers oxygen. The anchor sentence maintains forward momentum. Neither can be absent.

**Priority order for what holds the piece together:**

```
专业（底子）→ 观点（方向）→ 主线（绳子）→ 节奏（牵引力）
Expertise (foundation) → Viewpoint (direction) → Main line (rope) → Rhythm (traction)
```

---

### The Anchor Sentence（扣主线句）

An anchor sentence is a short line that pulls a drift back to the main argument. It does not need to be long — one sentence is enough. But it must appear frequently.

**What it does**: After a digression (an example, a historical aside, a case study), the anchor sentence signals "and that's why we were talking about this in the first place." Without it, readers feel lost and have to mentally re-trace the logic. When that happens, the flow breaks.

**The fatal mistake**: Drifting far from the main line and then yanking back hard. The reader has to spend cognitive effort re-following the logic. Heart rate goes up. Flow breaks. They consider closing the tab.

---

### The Wave Motion Pattern

Each section of an article follows a small wave:

1. **Push forward** on the main argument
2. **Drift slightly** — an example, a human story, a piece of background knowledge
3. **Anchor back** — one sentence connecting the drift to the main line
4. **Push forward** again at a slightly higher level

The emotional curve follows the same pattern: each drift should add something (curiosity, warmth, surprise), and each return should feel like "okay, and this is why that matters."

## Creative Case Methodology

When writing about product evaluations or tool recommendations, the article must include at least one creative case that produces a genuine "holy shit" reaction. The case is the proof of concept. Without it, the article is just a description.

### Three Types of Anchors

<CardGroup cols={3}>
  <Card title="Trending Anchor（热点锚点）" icon="fire">
    The current most viral movie, TV show, social event, or internet meme. Captures the largest possible common attention.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Pain Point Anchor（痛点锚点）" icon="bandage">
    The most frequent, most frustrating user problem. Practical value. Readers click because they recognize the pain.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Wow Factor Anchor（炫技点锚点）" icon="stars">
    The most paradigm-breaking core function of the AI tool itself. Technology awe. Readers share because it blew their mind.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

The strongest cases often combine two or even all three anchors.

---

### Packaging a Case as a Micro-Story

<Steps>

### Reveal the Challenge
What problem did you want to solve? What made you go try this? The challenge must be specific and relatable — not "I wanted to test the tool" but "I needed to batch-process 200 video thumbnails and had no idea how to do it without paying someone."

### Show the Creative Leap
What unconventional idea occurred to you? This is the "骚操作" (clever move) moment. The reader should think "I would never have thought of that."

### Demonstrate the Process
How did you actually do it, step by step? Include screenshots, the core Prompt, the moment things went wrong and how you fixed it. Show your work.

### Detonate the Result
The final "卧槽" (holy shit) output. The result must have independent display value, creative value, and inspirational value. If you're not excited about it yourself, don't publish it.

</Steps>

<Warning>
  If you're not genuinely excited by the case yourself, don't force it. "The standard: independent display value, creative value, and inspirational value. If you can't get yourself High on it, don't publish it."
</Warning>

---

### Example: The Escalating Showcase in Action

When testing multiple models or showing multiple use cases, apply the 升番 (escalation) logic. Never reveal the best result first. Build up:

> 比如Gemini 2.5 Pro，率先翻车。
> 给我扯了一堆有的没的，然后说了一句，圆圈。
> 圈你妹= =
>
> GPT-5-Thinking，想了2分多分钟，直接阵亡。
>
> 甚至，我还试了一下豪华版GPT-5 Pro。
> 在长达7分钟的花里胡哨之后，宣布直接躺平。

Each test is a small beat. The escalation creates an emotional arc. Weakest first. Most devastating saved for the end. The reader's anticipation keeps building — "okay but can ANY of them do it?" — and the eventual answer (or the continued failure) is all the more impactful for the buildup.
